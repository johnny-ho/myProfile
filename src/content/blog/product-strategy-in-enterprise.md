When I moved into enterprise product management, the biggest adjustment wasn't the complexity of the technology or the scale of the organization. It was recalibrating what "good" looks like.

In consumer products, speed is celebrated. Ship fast, learn fast, iterate. In enterprise — especially in an organization like TSMC — the calculus is different.

## The Stakes Are Different

A consumer app push that breaks the experience for 2% of users is a bug. A platform issue in a semiconductor fab management tool can affect wafer yield, which translates directly into hundreds of millions of dollars.

This changes how you think about risk. Not that you become risk-averse — you become *risk-aware*. You learn to ask: what's the cost of being wrong here? And you design your product process accordingly.

## Alignment Is the Product

In enterprise, the product itself is only part of the value delivered. How well it integrates into existing workflows, how much it reduces friction for operators, how well it's adopted across departments — these are all part of the product experience.

This means strategy is inherently cross-functional. You're not just defining features; you're defining change management, training, and adoption paths.

## What I've Learned

1. **Build trust before building features.** Stakeholders who trust your judgment give you more latitude to make bold calls.

2. **Slow down to speed up.** Taking two extra weeks to align stakeholders upfront can save months of rework downstream.

3. **Define success in business terms, not product terms.** "We shipped the feature" is not success. "Operator cycle time dropped by 15%" is.

Enterprise product work is slower, more political, and more nuanced than it looks from the outside. But done well, it's some of the most impactful product work you can do.

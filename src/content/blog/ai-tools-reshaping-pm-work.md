Over the past year, I've been intentionally experimenting with AI tools in my product management workflow — and the results have surprised me. Not because AI is magic, but because the leverage points are different from what I expected.

## Where AI Actually Helps

### 1. Research Synthesis

The most immediate win was in research. Synthesizing dozens of user interviews, support tickets, and market reports used to take days. With AI-assisted summarization, I can now generate a first draft of insights in hours — leaving more time for the critical thinking that machines can't do: deciding *what matters* and *why*.

### 2. Writing First Drafts

PRDs, feature specs, stakeholder summaries — AI is a remarkably good at generating first drafts. The key is learning how to prompt well and knowing that the value you add is in the editing and judgment, not the blank-page creation.

### 3. Challenging Your Own Thinking

This one surprised me most. Asking an AI to steelman a counterargument, or to critique your product strategy from a competitor's perspective, surfaces blind spots that are easy to miss when you're close to the work.

## Where It Doesn't Help (Yet)

AI is still weak at understanding the organizational and political dynamics that drive real product decisions. Knowing *when to push* on a stakeholder, when a technical constraint is a real constraint vs. a negotiating position — these require human judgment and contextual knowledge that AI doesn't have.

## My Current Setup

- **Claude** for writing, analysis, and thinking through complex problems
- **Cursor** for technical explorations and writing lightweight scripts
- **Notion AI** for meeting notes and document summarization

The tools change. The underlying skill — knowing what to ask and what to do with the answer — is what I'm investing in.
